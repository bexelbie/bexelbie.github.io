As I have been trying to re-learn how to write more frequently and consistently my stupid brain has been comparing me to others.

> Brain: [Paul Graham](http://paulgraham.com/index.html) writes great stuff, why can't you?
> Me: Maybe he's just better than ... wait.  In his [last post](http://paulgraham.com/greatwork.html) he thanked 19 people for help.

A lesson I am having to learn again, for the umpteenth time, is that there is often a team hidden in the background.  Paul Graham is definitely bright.  However, I am sure his first draft looked nothing like his third draft.  I am sure everyone one of those 19 people influenced his work in a way that resulted in material improvement.  It may have been a new angle, a forgotten fact, an additional anecdote or grammatical and phrasing clean up.  And while it doesn't detract from his work, it is important to remember he didn't do it all alone.

When you see an executive stand on stage at a keynote or talk in a company call there was a team that got them to where they are.  I was recently in the background on a live company call curating questions, that were then presented to potential speaker respondents to consider and be prepared to answer.  If you just watched the meeting it looked like these people got called on, were read a question cold, and then were brilliant.  That happens, just not as often as it seems to happen.

The moral of this story isn't, "comparisons are pointless because they have a team and you have you."  The real takeaway is **Get yourself a team.**

There are lots of people you know who will help you refine your ideas, discover holes in them, see a different perspective, or clean up your language.  All you have to do is ask!  All you have to do is follow these four simple steps:

1. Create something.  Make it worthwhile to consume/review, even if it isn't perfect.  It should likely be more than bullet points, but it doesn't have to be publication ready.
2. Ask someone to review it or explore it with you[^1].
3. Thank them profusely.
4. Lather, rinse, repeat (but not [literally](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lather,_rinse,_repeat)).

[^1]: If you run into Imposter Syndrome-style refusals such as, "I am not sure I have anything to add," or "that isn't my domain of expertise," hit them back with a request to be a [rubber duck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubber_duck_debugging). You'd be amazed at how much you learn about your ideas just by sharing them with a non-reactive listener.
